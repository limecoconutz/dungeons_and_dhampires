Three centuries of vampire rule have reshaped a continent. Here is what survived, what was erased, and what festers beneath the cobblestones.

The Principality of Veltharyn was not always ruled by the dead. Once it was a confederation of mortal city-states — trading partners who shared the Greyvein River and a mutual distrust of the mountain kingdoms to the north. That was before the Night of Falling Crowns, before the Crimson Compact, before Lord Valdris Umbrath dismantled the last mortal parliament and replaced its seats with the heads of those who had filled them.

That was three hundred and twelve years ago. The empire that rose in the ashes is elegant, brutal, and exhausted — and it does not yet know it is dying.

## I. Geography and the Lay of the Land

Veltharyn occupies a peninsula pressed between the Ashen Mountains to the north, the Saltwreck Sea to the west, and the Sunken Marches to the south — a natural fortress that has shaped its politics as much as its warfare. The terrain is varied and often hostile: dense pine forests riddled with fog, volcanic hot-spring basins that steam year-round, and limestone cliff coasts gnawed by the sea. It is a country that does not welcome travel, and its rulers have made no effort to change that.

The climate is perpetually overcast — not supernatural in origin (though many mortals believe so), but a product of the Ashen Mountains' rain shadow colliding with warm coastal air. For the vampire nobility, this is an extraordinary gift. Direct sunlight is a rarity in Veltharyn. Most seasons see no more than twelve sun-hours per week.

⚫ The Obsidian Spires

The northern highlands, home to the great noble keeps. Seven fortresses of black volcanic stone rise from the ridge like broken teeth. Each belongs to one of the sire houses. The roads between them are maintained but unlit — no torches, no lanterns. The Spires operate on the assumption that only those with night-sight travel at speed.

🌫️ The Ashen Moors

A vast central plateau of peat bog and silver-birch forest. Nominally uncontrolled territory, the Moors are where the mortal resistance operates — and where old things sleep beneath the waterlogged earth. DMs should treat the Moors as the campaign's wilderness, a place where none of the empire's rules apply and the ecology is alien.

🏙️ Duskport, the Mortal Quarter

The empire's only functioning port city, Duskport is mortal-administered by necessity — the nobles have no interest in shipping manifests. It is loud, grimy, alive in a way nowhere else in Veltharyn is, and absolutely riddled with spies from every faction. The black market here trades in blood vials, sunstones, and forged bloodline papers.

📚 The Sunken Library

Once the great university of the mortal confederation, now partially submerged after the Greyvein was redirected during the Compact Wars. The upper floors remain accessible and are technically neutral ground. The lower floors breathe, according to the few who have returned from them.

## II. History: The Three Ages of Veltharyn

### The Mortal Age (Before Year 0)

Pre-Compact Veltharyn had no unified name. Historians within the empire — all of whom are vampires or their appointed agents — call this period the Long Slumber, which tells you everything about their perspective. It was, by most surviving accounts, a messy, quarrelsome, surprisingly literate civilization. The city-states of Duskport, Caldenmere, and Thresh maintained rival academies, rival navies, and rival philosophies. They were well on their way to industrialization when Valdris Umbrath came down from the mountains.

### The Compact Era (Year 0–180)

The Crimson Compact is the foundational legal document of Veltharyn — a treaty signed between the six vampire sire houses (under duress, in several cases) and the mortal population (under significantly more duress). The terms were simple: mortals would provide blood tithe and labor; the houses would provide order and protection from the mountain threats that had plagued the region for decades. The arrangement was never described as coercion in official records. It was called a "covenant of mutual flourishing."

> "The Compact gave us peace. The kind of peace a fly enjoys when the spider stops moving."— Author unknown; graffiti found on Duskport's harbor wall, repeatedly painted over

### The Current Age: Year 312 and the Fracture

Something is wrong in Veltharyn. The sire houses have not openly fought in sixty years, which should be a sign of stability — instead it feels like held breath. Three houses have recalled their outer-region emissaries. One has stopped producing dhampire wards entirely. The blood tithe quota was quietly raised by fifteen percent last winter, and the official reason was never given.

The mortal resistance, long a scattered nuisance, has united under a single anonymous signal: a red candle placed in a window means you are safe to speak. No one knows who started it. The Crimson Council does not know either, which terrifies them far more than they will admit.

## III. Political Structure: The Crimson Council

Veltharyn is technically governed by the Crimson Council — a body of representatives from each of the six sire houses plus a Mortal Liaison position that has been filled by the same family for four generations and has no actual voting power. In practice, the Council is a cold war dressed in velvet. Each house controls a geographic region, a set of industries, and a number of dhampire wards who serve as its agents in mortal spaces.

The dhampires — that is, the player characters — occupy an unusual position in this system. They are simultaneously assets (valued for their ability to walk in daylight, blend with mortals, and perform tasks that fully-turned vampires cannot), weapons (trained from adolescence in their house's combat tradition), and vulnerabilities (anyone who turns a dhampire against their sire house gains leverage). The houses know this. The player characters, if they are clever, know it too.

DM Note: Running Veltharyn

The most important thing to establish in early sessions is that Veltharyn feels old and tired, not just evil and oppressive. The vampire nobility is not uniformly monstrous — some are aesthetes, some are genuinely fond of their dhampire wards, some are exhausted idealists who believed the Compact would work. Let players encounter a vampire who is kind, interesting, even sympathetic. Then make the system they uphold visible. The horror of Veltharyn is structural, not personal.

## IV. Religion and the Divine in Veltharyn

The gods did not abandon Veltharyn — several of them appear to have made a pragmatic peace with it. The Dawnmother's clergy was dismantled in Year 12 (sunlight worship being, understandably, politically inconvenient), but the Pale Shepherd's church remains tolerated: death-priests who service the populace's need to bury their dead provide a social function the houses cannot. The Threadweavers — keepers of fate and consequence — are watched but permitted, perhaps because even vampires find the idea of angering fate to be unwise.

Dhampire characters with religious backgrounds will find their faith complicated by their nature. The Dawnmother's blessings still function — a dhampire paladin can still channel divine power — but the sun her clergy once worshipped burns their skin. The gods are not commenting on the contradiction. They rarely do.

## V. The World Beyond the Peninsula

Veltharyn is sealed. Not physically — ships still arrive in Duskport — but diplomatically and culturally. The outside world knows little of what happens behind the Ashen Mountains, and what it knows is shaped by the empire's carefully maintained facade: a stable, wealthy, unusual-but-prosperous monarchy with eccentric noble customs. Several outside nations trade happily with Duskport. None of them ask why Veltharyn's nobles never attend daytime meetings.

For the campaign, this matters primarily because it defines the shape of escape. If the players want to run — if they reject the premise of working within the empire entirely — Duskport's harbor is the only exit. And the harbor masters all answer to [[House Lacrimae\|House Lacrimae]]. Each player belongs to one of [[The Six Blodlines\|The Six Blodlines]]
